Handling a Red/Adverse Background Verification Report – A Step‑by‑Step HR Guide

An adverse BGV report doesn’t automatically mean a rejection. Here’s a clear, fair process for HR teams to verify the findings, communicate with the candidate, and make a legally sound decision.

📅 July 2026 ⏱ 6 min read 👤 Truevence Editorial Team

Step 1 – Don’t Panic, Verify the Finding

Red flags are not always accurate. Start by confirming the discrepancy directly with your BGV provider. Request the original source documentation and timeline of the finding. At Truevence, we provide granular details – institution name, contact person, and the exact mismatch – so you can act with confidence.

  • ✓ Ask for the verification closure report.
  • ✓ Check if the discrepancy is factual or just incomplete.
  • ✓ Determine if it’s a minor date mismatch or a serious fake document.

Step 2 – Communicate with the Candidate

Give the candidate a chance to explain. Many adverse reports result from administrative errors – a university name change, a previous employer’s HR who never updated records. Send a written notification (email is fine) outlining the specific discrepancy and request supporting documents. Set a deadline (typically 5-7 business days).

Step 3 – Re‑Verification or Escalation

If the candidate provides counter‑evidence, initiate a targeted re‑verification. At Truevence, we prioritize such reviews at no extra charge within a dispute window. If the evidence supports the candidate, we update the report to “Clear.” If the original red flag holds, move to Step 4.

Step 4 – Make the Decision & Document It

If the adverse finding is confirmed and material to the role (e.g., fake degree for a teaching position), you may decide to revoke the offer or terminate employment. Ensure your decision is based on a consistent policy applied to all candidates. Document the reasons clearly, and never use a minor discrepancy (like a 2‑month date gap) as a pretext for rejection without proper evaluation.

When to Seek Legal Advice

If the adverse finding involves a criminal record that doesn’t directly impact job duties, consult your legal team before making a decision. India’s right to privacy and employment laws are evolving – a blanket “no criminal record” policy can be challenged.
